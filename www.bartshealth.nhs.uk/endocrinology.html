Endocrinology

Welcome to Barts Health Endocrinology Service. We are one of a few hospital trusts in the country to offer treatment and care for some of the rarer endocrine conditions (hormone disorders). We have some of Britain’s leading specialists on our team. As some of our services and facilities are so specialised, GPs from all over the UK and across Europe refer patients to Barts Health.

Services - Services

Welcome to Barts Health Endocrinology Service. We are one of a few hospital trusts in the country to offer treatment and care for some of the rarer endocrine conditions (hormone disorders). We have some of Britain’s leading specialists on our team. As some of our services and facilities are so specialised, GPs from all over the UK and across Europe refer patients to Barts Health.

We have strong links with the oncology service which allows us to take a multidisciplinary approach to patient care and enables us to successfully treat some of the most rare and complex cases.

With our own dedicated endocrine investigation and inpatient unit we lead and participate in clinical and laboratory based studies and trials to further understand about endocrine tumours and their treatment and teaching programmes within the department.

We run endocrinology services from:

  • St Bartholomew’s Hospital
  • Newham University Hospital
  • Whipps Cross University Hospital

For patients

For an appointment with the Barts Health endocrinology team, please ask your GP for a referral.

Endocrine disorders may be difficult to diagnose and some conditions are rare. To describe endocrinology in simple terms, various glands release a variety of hormones which play important roles in how the body functions. If any of these glands over-produce or under-produce hormones, or if they grow and develop tumours within them, this can lead to a variety of health problems.

Conditions we treat

  • Endocrine cancers
  • Thyroid disorders
  • Parathyroid disorders
  • Pituitary gland disorders
  • Adrenal disorders
  • Neuroendocrine tumours
  • Multiple endocrine neoplasia syndromes (MEN1 and MEN2)
  • Von Hippel Lindau syndrome
  • Other inherited endocrine conditions: Carney complex, McCune-Albright syndrome
  • SDH (Succinate Dehydrogenase) mutation
  • Cancer late effects
  • Thalassaemia late effects

Types of endocrine cancer and treatments

Thyroid

Differentiated thyroid cancer 
There are two types of differentiated thyroid cancer: papillary and follicular. For the majority of patients with differentiated thyroid cancer, treatment will involve surgery and radioactive iodine therapy, together with long term support and follow up.  Every year we administer approximately 100 doses of radioactive iodine for patients with differentiated thyroid cancer.

Medullary thyroid cancer 
This is a very rare type of cancer. The most effective treatment is surgery. Some patients will need radiotherapy, chemotherapy and radionuclide therapy. For some patients the cancer may be caused by a genetic mutation which can be inherited and this possibility will be considered.

Anaplastic thyroid cancer 
Anaplastic thyroid cancer is another very rare type of thyroid cancer. Surgery, radiotherapy and insertion of a tracheostomy may all play a role for certain patients.

Parathyroid
Parathyroid glands are attached to the back of the thyroid gland which, in turn, sits at the front of the neck. Tumours of the parathyroid glands cause high calcium which can lead to problems with your bones and kidneys. Most parathyroid tumours are not cancerous but do require thorough and careful evaluation and imaging. This is because they are very small glands and accurate localisation is essential to help guide effective/curative surgery. Occasionally the gland enlargement can be cancerous and treatment may, in addition to surgery, include radiotherapy and a new drug called cinacalcet.

Pituitary
The pituitary gland is a small gland at the base of the brain which is vital for controlling many of the hormones in the body. Tumours affecting the gland are usually benign (non-cancerous) and small. Most commonly the tumours make a hormone called prolactin and these tumours can be controlled by medication alone. Larger tumours can cause problems of:

  • local effects due to their size, particularly affecting vision
  • overproduction of certain hormones (eg the conditions of acromegaly and Cushing’s disease)
  • under-production of certain hormones

The endocrine cancer centre is one of only a few centres in the UK to have all the necessary tools for dealing with the larger more complextumours on one site; these include complex endocrine studies, dedicated imaging, neurosurgery, radiotherapy and gamma knife radiosurgery. Our surgeons routinely perform pituitary surgery using an endoscopic approach and the gamma knife centre now treats complex pituitary tumours from around the country. Many patients have their initial assessments and surgery here with multi-disciplinary input with subsequent follow up with your local endocrinologist.

Adrenal

Adrenal lesions 
Adrenal lesions most are non cancerous but need careful evaluation to sure they are not producing excessive amounts of steroid hormones and to ensure that their characteristics are benign. Adrenal conditions that we treat include congenital adrenal hyperplasia, hyperaldosteronism, Conn’s syndrome, Addison’s disease, Cushing’s syndrome.

Adrenal cancer
Adrenal cancer is a very rare condition and may, in some cases, be associated with excessive production of certain steroid hormones. Surgery, chemotherapy and radiotherapy may all be used for the treatment of this rare type of cancer. Patients with the possibility of this type of cancer will undergo extensive investigation and imaging in order to help guide treatment decisions.

Phaeochromocytoma and paragangliomas 
Phaeochromocytoma and paragangliomas are rare tumours of the autonomic nervous system which usually arise in the adrenal glands but may occur at othere sites in the body. The tumours may make the hormones adrenaline and noradrenaline which can cause many symptoms and elevated blood pressure. They are usually non-cancerous but can be malignant in 10% of cases. A proportion of people may develop the tumours as part of an underlying genetic condition and careful assessment, localization and treatment prior to surgical removal are essential.

Neuroendocrine tumours
Neuroendocrine tumours (NETs) are a group of tumours that start in the cells of the neuroendocrine system. These tumours most commonly arise from cells in the lung, bowel or pancreas. They may make hormones which can cause problems such as diarrhoea. Treatment is aimed at controlling these symoptoms in addition to the size and/or spread of the NET. Treatment may include surgery, radiofrequency ablation (heating of the tumour until it dies), hepatic embolisation (blocking blood supply to parts of the liver), chemotherapy, radiotherapy and radio-nuclide therapy. In addition, treatments to control the hormone secretion may be necessary.

Multiple endocrine neoplasia syndromes (MEN1 and MEN2)
This is a familial, inherited condition with the development of at least two endocrine tumours or lesions. They can be of malignant or benign nature. MEN1 includes pituitary adenomas, parathyroid hyperplasia, angiofibromas and pancreatic tumours. MEN2 is split into MEN2A (medullary thyroid cancer, parathyroid hyperplasia and phaeochromocytoma) and MEN2B (medullary thyroid cancer, neuromas, phaeochromocytoma and marfanoid body habitus). We work very closely with our geneticist and we usually look after family populations with this condition with early monitoring of family members at risk.

Succinate dehydrogenase mutation
This is a rare inherited condition causing phaeochromocytomas and paragangliomas, of which one third could be malignant. Patients are monitored regularly with blood and urine tests as well as radiological imaging and some lesions will need to be excised under the expert hands of our endocrine surgeons.

Von Hippel-Lindau disease
A very rare Inherited condition that predisposes to benign cysts in the kidneys and pancreas which has the potential to become malignant. Other tumours include haemangioblastomas found along the central nervous system and in the eye. They also develop phaeochromocytomas and pancreatic neuroendocrine tumours.

Late effects of cancer treatment
Recent evidence shows that some of the treatments used for cancer both in children and in adults can have late effects on various aspects of health including the endocrine system. We offer a specialist joint clinic to evaluate and treat patients who have or may be at risk of these conditions.

Thalassaemia late effects
Thalassaemia major is a serious condition that require multiple blood transfusion. Many patients develop endocrine problems including reduction in sex hormones, growth failure, diabetes, hypothyroidism and poor bone health. This is thought to be a result of the multiple blood transfusion leading to iron deposition in endocrine organs. These patients need to followed up regularly and replace any hormones that are deficient.

  • Thyroid One Stop Clinic
  • Radioiodine for thyroid disease clinic [link to ‘our services’]

  • Endocrine Antenatal clinic
  • Specialist Endocrine nurse for pituitary tumours
  • Specialist Endocrine nurse for thyroid cancers
  • Specialist Endocrine nurse for patients with VHL and SDH mutations
  • Endocrine Day case Unit

  • Venous sampling (for pituitary, adrenal and parathyroid conditions)
  • Nuclear medicine scans (I-123, MIBG, Octreotide, sestaMIBI)
  • DEXA and PET scan

Non-invasive treatment

  • Radionuclide therapies for patients with neuroendocrine tumours
  • Radioiodine therapies for thyroid disease including thyrotoxicosis, multinodular goitres and thyroid cancers
  • Non-invasive radiosurgery (known as ‘gamma knife’) for patients with pituitary tumours.

What to expect

For your outpatient appointment

Once you receive your first appointment with us, this appointment will be for blood test to ensure the results will be ready for your consultation with your doctor. The blood test will take place on Francis Fraser ward, 2nd floor, East Wing, Barts Hospital. After your blood test, you will then be given an appointment to see the doctor and the appointment will take place in the Outpatients building on the 1st floor.

For your inpatient stay

Our patients are admitted on a planned basis for investigations and management.  Patients who are due to be admitted will be asked to attend a pre-admission visit a few weeks before.  This is an afternoon visit where our doctors will take a thorough history and examination and ensure that all the investigations required are booked for the inpatient period. 

Staying with us during your tests

If you are having tests at Barts Hospital (spread over a few days) and do not live near the hospital, you are welcome to stay at Gloucester House Hostel.
This newly-refurbished hostel, within the grounds of Barts Hospital, provides free overnight accommodation for patients requiring a series of tests but live far from the hospital. There are five bedrooms available for various hospital services, such as endocrinology, and ten bedrooms dedicated to cancer patients.

Accommodation comprises single rooms equipped with wash-hand basins, a television and tea/coffee making facilities. There are separate bathrooms for male and female patients, and a shared kitchen for the preparation of meals, with an on-site housekeeper service.

If you stay at the hostel, you will be given contact details for staff in case you become unwell at any time. If necessary, you will be admitted and given a hospital bed.

Why choose us

We offer very specialist care and treatment for all patients with endocrine conditions, seeing over 2000 new patients a year. We have a dedicated endocrine investigation unit and inpatient ward run by a specialised team of nurses. 

For endocrine cancers, which are very rare, we see and treat approximately 200 new cases per year. All our cases are discussed in our multi-disciplinary team meeting, incorporating clinical expertise for all aspects of the disease with the use of state of the art facilities. The multi-disciplinary team includes endocrinologists, dedicated surgeons, oncologists, radiologist, nuclear medicine physicians, pathologists, biochemists, geneticists and specialist endocrine and cancer nurses.
